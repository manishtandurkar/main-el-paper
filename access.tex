\begin{abstract}
Climate change is causing a shift of agroclimatic zones toward hotter and drier conditions, and is threatening more than 600 million livelihoods in India that depend on agriculture. Current crop failure studies are based on static agroclimatic classifications or univariate stress indices, neither of which include consideration of the capacity of groundwater irrigation to mitigate stresses nor clearly mark the point at which failure results in permanent loss of viability. We introduce VegShift, a multi-source geospatial framework for identifying the Crop Viability Loss Event (CVLE): A formally dated year that is defined by a city-crop pair experiencing atmospheric rainfall failure, hydrological recharge collapse, and at least two of three acute phenological stress conditions. We generate a 250-sample master dataset of atmospheric, groundwater and rainfed crop suitability data across 25 years (2000-2024) over a collection of 19 (7.6\%) confirmed CVLE events in 10 major cities across India using daily atmospheric data from Open-Meteo, seasonal groundwater data from the Central Groundwater Board of India (CGBI) in 2,759 observation wells, and FAO Global Agro-Ecological Zones v4 (GAEZ v4) rainfed crop suitability rasters. Eight models (Logistic Regression, Random Forest, XGBoost, LightGBM, LSTM, TCN, Vanilla Transformer, and the Temporal Fusion Transformer) are tested and evaluated in a strict chronological train-test split. Logistic Regression achieves AUC-ROC=1.00 and Random Forest AUC-ROC=0.97, compared to all of the Deep Sequential Architectures, the TFT gives an AUC-ROC of 0.07. When analyzing the predictive signal, the feature group ablation shows that more than 98\% comes from the static context (FAO suitability class and K\"{o}ppen-Geiger zone encoding). The results of city analysis using the Control method indicate very low false detection rates for climatically stable cities, which confirms the validity of the dual-deficit CVLE formulation as a reliable event timestamping system.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\PARstart{A}{griculture} is the primary support of India's economy, which has almost 1.4 billion people and a workforce of around 45 percent of the total population, accounting for nearly 18 percent of the Gross Domestic Product (GDP) of the country \cite{fao2023}. However, this sector is highly susceptible to climate variability and total food grain production varies by as much as 20\% from drought to flood years and projections consistently show that South Asia is one of the areas where anthropogenic climate change will have a high impact on food grain production \cite{ipccar6wg2}. One vulnerability that has received relatively little attention is the "gradual but permanent" change in established agroclimatic zones, in which a city's climate conditions where a specific crop previously growable move quietly into a climate regime where the crop can no longer complete its life cycle. These are slow-onset climate transitions that build up over decades, and are not identified until agricultural failures have taken hold, whereas abrupt climate shocks call for immediate policy response.

Although K\"{o}ppen zones have been widely applied to agroclimatic mapping, e.g., \cite{beck2018} they are assumed to be static or are only used to stratify the geographical space at a coarse level for crop failure studies. The consequence is a significant gap: formalization of the year when a city's climate zone change results in an irreversible loss of crop viability.

Adding to this imbalance, current strategies for predicting crop failures primarily use a single dimension of stress indices (rainfall departure, temperature anomaly, or Standardised Precipitation Index) that are incapable of reflecting the dual-channel agricultural water supply in Indian cities \cite{lobell2007}. Rainfall shortfalls are cushioned by groundwater irrigation for the short term, but if recharge is lacking at the same time as the monsoon is failing, this buffer is absent. This interaction cannot be reflected by any single indicator in the atmosphere; a multi-source hydrologically based definition of the loss of crop viability is needed.

Our three main contributions to addressing these gaps in this paper are the formalisation of the \textit{Crop Viability Loss Event} (CVLE), which is a dual-deficit event definition, that is both the atmospheric rainfall failure and the groundwater recharge collapse must occur in consecutive years, and that at least two of three crop-specific phenological stress conditions must be observed. \textit{Second} we create a 250 sample, longitudinal data set of ten major cities in India over 25 years (2000--2024) by merging three heterogeneous sources: daily atmospheric climate data from Open-Meteo, CGWB seasonal groundwater well observations, and FAO GAEZ v4 rainfed crop suitability rasters. \textit{Third} we systematically compare eight machine learning and deep learning models such as Temporal Fusion Transformer, Random Forest, Logistic Regression, XGBoost, LightGBM, LSTM, TCN, and Vanilla Transformer, all with uncertainty quantification by Monte Carlo dropout and tree variance, feature group ablation across five mutually exclusive groups, and pairwise Wilcoxon significance testing across 28 model pairs.

The remainder of this paper is organised as follows. Section~\ref{sec:related_work} provides an overview of related work in four domains: K\"{o}ppen-Geiger classification in agriculture, crop failure prediction, interaction of groundwater and agriculture, and temporal deep learning for climate applications. The study area, data sources, and data pre-processing pipelines are outlined in Section~\ref{sec:data}. Section~\ref{sec:methodology} details the CVLE definition, feature engineering, model architectures, and evaluation protocol. The experimental results are reported in Section~\ref{sec:results} and findings, limitations, and threats to the validity of the results are discussed in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} provides guidelines for deployment and future research areas.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{K\"{o}ppen-Geiger Classification in Agriculture}
The K\"{o}ppen-Geiger (KG) climate classification was initially proposed by Wladimir K\"{o}ppen and improved successive versions by Peel \cite{peel2007} and Beck \cite{beck2018} aims to divide the earth's land area into five climate groups: tropical (\textit{A}), arid (\textit{B}), temperate (\textit{C}), continental (\textit{D}), and polar (\textit{E}) types which are distinguished by thresholds of mean monthly temperature and precipitation. It is the most popular framework for agroclimic zoning globally, due to its intuitive hierarchical nature and empirical match with vegetation and agricultural boundaries.

In agricultural science, the use of the KG zones has proved to be quite extensive, for example to outline crop suitability envelopes, crop simulation model calibration, and global food security evaluations \cite{zabel2014}. The Global Agro-Ecological Zones (GAEZ) of the Food and Agriculture Organisation (FAO) classify the suitability of crops in part by the thermal regime and moisture regime of the area, as derived from KG; and large-scale crop model ensembles, such as DSSAT and APSIM, routinely stratify by KG zone, where appropriate, to ensure climatic homogeneity within each simulation region. The zone is considered a stable boundary condition, and an area classified as \textit{BSh} (hot semi-arid) in 1980 remains in that classification for the entire study period; crop management recommendations are based on this classification.

The static assumption is, however, no longer sustainable in an anthropogenic climate change. Under RCP~8.5, Rubel and Kottek \cite{rubel2010} showed that almost one-third of the land area of the Earth could be reclassified as KG by 2100. For the Indian subcontinent, the observed aridification of cities such as in Rajasthan-Gujarat is already bringing these cities closer to the hyper-arid \textit{BWh} climate and the erratic intensification of monsoon events poses a threat to cities in peninsular India that are classified as \textit{Aw} or \textit{Am}. However, none of these studies has been formally connected with year-to-year transitions in the KG zone at a city scale and binary outcomes for crop viability. VegShift fills this gap by calculating the KG zone each year from the actual climate data, identifying long-term transitions using a stability filter over three years of data, and linking transitions with the CVLE timestamps.

\subsection{Crop Failure Prediction}
Over the last 20 years, the prediction of crop failure has become more and more complex over the years, moving from basic agronomic thresholds to more advanced machine learning pipelines. Early drought studies used univariate drought indices, such as the Standardised Precipitation Index (SPI), Palmer Drought Severity Index and the rainfall departure from climatological mean, to determine whether a growing season was a drought failure or not \cite{lobell2007}. These indices are easy to compute and easy to understand to agricultural extension workers, but they lack good discriminative power in situations where farmers can use groundwater irrigation instead of monsoon shortfall. There is no single threshold level for rainfall that would allow for separation between rainfall shortfalls that result in crop failure and rainfall shortfalls that can be fully made up with water withdrawals from aquifers.

This limitation has been partially overcome by the use of ensemble machine learning techniques which have allowed multi-variate feature fusion. The combination of satellite remote sensing indices (NDVI, EVI) with soil properties and multi-variate climate drivers with Random Forest and gradient boosting models have been used to yield strong performance in crop yield regression analysis \cite{crane2018,you2017}. The skill of forecasts has also been enhanced at regional and national scales using deep learning architectures such as CNNs applied to gridded weather fields, and LSTM-based yield forecasters \cite{wang2020}. Most of these studies however consider the prediction problem as a continuous yield regression problem, instead of as a rare binary event classification problem, and optimize the mean absolute error for annual production volumes. The difference is key: a Crop Viability Loss Event (in which the crop cannot recover and the event is irreversible) is detected through maximizing the recall of the positive class, not by minimizing the average prediction error for the yield.

In the literature, most existing binary crop failure classification algorithms rely on threshold systems. Water stress indicators, crop suitability scores, and crop water requirements deficit indicators are all empirical, with pre-set thresholds for viability and non-viability of city-crop pairs \cite{fao2023}. These thresholds are based on the literature on agrophysiology around the world, but are applied in a fixed manner, rather than being adapted to the local hydrology or being learned from longitudinal data on observations. VegShift goes against this trend by learning from 25 years of multi-source time-series the event boundaries, and then applying the optimal decision threshold search for each of eight models on the validation set and evaluating the best performance range from linear discriminants to attention-based temporal architectures according to a uniform evaluation protocol.

\subsection{Groundwater Depletion and Agricultural Interaction}
India has the largest groundwater withdrawal in the world, with an estimated 230--250~km$^3$ withdrawn annually, more than a quarter of the global total, mainly for agricultural irrigation, \cite{famiglietti2014}. This dependency was even further enhanced in the era of the Green Revolution when the varieties of crops grown were higher yielding and needed more consistent water inputs over the entire season than could be delivered by the monsoon. By observing the changes in total terrestrial water storage, GRACE satellite gravity measurements have documented a statistically significant decrease in TTS over northern India between 2002 and 2008, which is mainly due to groundwater overextraction in the Indo-Gangetic Plain at the rate of $-17.7 \pm 1.0$~km$^3$~yr$^{-1}$ \cite{rodell2009}. The Central Groundwater Board (CGWB) monitoring network, which includes over 15,000 observation wells, shows that the groundwater table has been falling in these states and some districts in the Trans-Gangetic Plain at 2~m~yr$^{-1}$ \cite{cgwb2022}.

The impact of using up an aquifer is always non-linear on the farm. Groundwater, which is economically available, is used by farmers in addition to monsoon rainfall and can hide the stress on crops. The failure is not due to insufficient rainfall, rather it is due to failure of recharge, the source of water for the system, and to failure of the hydrological buffer. Singh \textit{et al.} \cite{singh2009} demonstrated that in northwest India, groundwater depletion has already jeopardized the sustainability of wheat production in those districts where pre-monsoon groundwater levels are more than 30~m below ground level, and Qureshi \textit{et al.} \cite{qureshi2010} further showed that in Punjab, over 70\% of crop water demand is being met by groundwater irrigation, and therefore, groundwater recharge failure is synonymous with monsoon failure from an agronomic perspective.

However, this knowledge is not reflected in most of the currently available crop failure prediction models based on ML, which are primarily based on surface meteorological reanalysis data. VegShift addresses this by combining seasonal well observations from CGWB (2,759 well monitoring stations across the ten study cities) to calculate aquifer health indicators for each city (pre-monsoon depth, depletion rate, and monsoon recharge efficiency), and adding these indicators as first-class variables to the CVLE dual-deficit model. The empirical validation of this integration comes from the feature group ablation experiments, where the single-group LSTM classification model achieves the highest AUC value (0.8621) with hydrological features, indicating that recurrent architectures have the capability to make the most of the groundwater depletion trends as a lagged viability signal.

\subsection{Temporal Deep Learning for Climate Applications}
In recent years, use of deep learning in climate time-series analysis has shown an explosive growth in response to the availability of high-resolution gridded reanalysis datasets and the success of attention models and causal-convolutional networks in learning non-linear temporal structures that are hard for traditional statistical models to represent at such scales and complexity. The first deep architectures to show skill on multi-variate climate problems were Long Short-Term Memory networks \cite{hochreiter1997} which addressed the vanishing gradient problem, a problem common to vanilla recurrent networks. These LSTM models have recently been used to predict streamflow flows \cite{kratzert2018} and temperature downscaling, and forecasted agricultural yields with similar performance to process-based models. LSTMs, however, are inherently sequential, which means they are not parallelisable and can be less effective in learning long-range dependencies in datasets that have a cyclical agronomic structure.

In order to allow for fully parallelizable training, while preserving strict causality (no information leakage from future timesteps), Temporal Convolutional Networks (TCN) \cite{bai2018} are based on causal dilated convolutions. The exponential dilation schedule, where $d = 2^i$ for every layer, enables the effective receptive field of the TCN to increase exponentially with depth, yielding good results on data with long-range seasonal structures, while avoiding problems with gradient propagation. Various multi-variate time-series classification tasks have been compared against TCNs and LSTM, with consistently good results for TCNs, especially when tackled with structured data having periodic components \cite{bai2018}. More recently, transformer architectures based on natural language processing (using positional encodings) have shown even better performance for long-horizon climate forecasting and classification of extreme weather events, where each output position can attend to all input positions at once \cite{vaswani2017}.

The most architecturally advanced of these models is the Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT) \cite{lim2021} that has gated residual networks for variable selection, local sequence encoders (LSTM), and multi-head self-attention for long-range dependency modelling, and generates interpretable multi-horizon quantile forecasts. TFT is a state-of-the-art solution to electricity demand forecasting, retail sales forecasting, and traffic flow forecasting, with hundreds of thousands of samples used for training. Its use, however, has not yet been investigated on small longitudinal datasets, like the CVLE corpus with just 250 samples, for two-class classification of binary events. Rolnick \textit{et al.} \cite{rolnick2022} surveyed machine learning solutions for tackling climate change in various fields and pointed out data scarcity and extreme class imbalance as open challenges for deep temporal models in agriculture. VegShift is the first systematic assessment of TFT for rare binary classification of an event in a climate-agricultural domain, showing that the objective QuantileLoss and the model's $\sim$115k parameters were not well suited to a 250-sample binary classification problem, which is a warning to practitioners using TFT for small-scale geospatial studies.

\subsection{4B. Annual Feature Engineering}

We build a feature space of two groups of features: 17 time-invariant and 5 static features, which capture climate shifts, groundwater depletion, and crop phenology, respectively.

\subsubsection{1. Atmospheric and Climate Features}

These characteristics represent the annual energy, moisture and baseline dry season characteristics:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{temp\_mean \& temp\_max}: Average of the maximum and minimum temperatures over a given day (C) (over the year).

    \item \textbf{rainfall\_annual}: Total annual precipitation (mm).

    \item \textbf{wind\_speed}: Annual mean of the daily maximum wind speed (m/s) (3.6 divides the value of wind speed).

    \item \textbf{humidity}: Relative humidity (\%) estimated using Steadman's apparent temperature inversion. Actual vapor pressure is calculated as
    \[
    e = \frac{AT_{\text{mean}} - T_{\text{mean}} + 0.70 \times wind_{ms} + 4.00}{0.33}
    \]
    saturation vapor pressure as
    \[
    e_s = 6.1078 \times \exp\left(\frac{17.27 \times T_{\text{mean}}}{237.3 + T_{\text{mean}}}\right)
    \]
    and relative humidity as
    \[
    RH = \operatorname{clip}\left(\frac{e}{e_s} \times 100,\;5,\;100\right).
    \]

    \item \textbf{n\_dry\_months}: The number of dry months in a year (month with rainfall of less than 60 mm).
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{2. Phenological and Agro-Climatic Features}

These simulate the window requirements and the cropping requirements:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{monsoon\_onset\_doy}: First day-of-year (after DOY 121 / May 1st) during which 5-day cumulative rainfall is greater than 25 mm and on that day, at least 3 rainy days (IMD standard).

    \item The sowing window miss, which is the difference between the sowing window of the crop, calculated as the difference between the day of the monsoon onset and the optimal sowing date, divided by 30.

    \item \textbf{gdd\_accumulation}: accumulated GDD from April to September:
    \[
    \sum \max(T_{\text{daily\_mean}} - T_{\text{base}},0)
    \]

    \item \textbf{gdd\_adequate}: Binary flag that is true when
    \[
    gdd_{\text{accumulation}} \ge gdd_{\min}.
    \]

    \item \textbf{crop\_water\_deficit}: Fractional precipitation shortfall:
    \[
    \frac{water_{\text{req}} - rainfall_{\text{annual}}}
         {water_{\text{req}}}.
    \]
\dots
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{3. Hydrological and Aquifer Features}

From observation wells in a 50 km radius from the city's centroid:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{pre\_monsoon\_depth\_mbgl}: Median groundwater level in metres below ground (mbgl) measured in May.

    \item \textbf{depletion\_rate}: the change in January groundwater depth from the previous year (YoY change in ground water depth:
    \[
    Jan\_depth(t) - Jan\_depth(t-1)
    \]
    )

    \item \textbf{recharge\_efficiency}: This is the aquifer rise per unit of rain
    \[
    \frac{May\_depth - August\_depth}
         {rainfall_{\text{annual}}}.
    \]

    \item \textbf{dual\_deficit}: binary variable that is 1 when the atmospheric failure (crop\_water\_deficit) and hydrological failure (recharge\_efficiency) occur simultaneously; 0 otherwise.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{4. Static Context and Cyclicity Features}

These are geographical baseline, crop thresholds and cyclical indicators:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{GAEZ crop suitability score (1-7)} refers to the \textbf{gaez\_baseline\_class}: Static baseline (1981-2010).

    \item \textbf{koppen\_zone\_enc}: Annual Köppen-Geiger zone, in integer encoding.

    \item \textbf{gdd\_min}: Minimum gdd needed for each crop.

    \item \textbf{water\_req}: Water requirement by each crop.

    \item \textbf{cycle\_sin\_5yr \& cycle\_cos\_5yr}: 5-year periodic cycle signals for temporal models:
    \[
    \sin\left(\frac{2\pi \times year\_index}{5}\right)
    \]
    \[
    \cos\left(\frac{2\pi \times year\_index}{5}\right).
    \]
\end{itemize}

\subsection{4C. Crop Viability Loss Event (CVLE) Definition}

Crop failure is generally modelled in traditional agricultural risk assessments with fixed agro-climatic zones or single variable indices like the Standardized Precipitation Index (SPI). These approaches do not, however, take into account the adaptive capacity of agricultural systems, in this case groundwater irrigation if rain fails; nor are they able to represent non-stationary trends caused by climate change.

To overcome these, a new, localized approach is proposed called the Crop Viability Loss Event (CVLE). CVLE is a formally dated, year in which a specific crop species cultivation becomes permanently non-viable in a city because simultaneous failure of atmospheric rainfall inputs and groundwater recharge buffers, plus acute phenological stress.

\subsubsection{1) The Dual-Deficit Mechanism}

The "dual-deficit" mechanism is at the core of the CVLE framework. But when there is a deficit in annual rainfall, farmers can tap the groundwater reserves to make up for the deficiency and therefore a shortfall does not immediately mean crop failure. But there's a limit to this buffering ability. A dual-deficit is a critical, simultaneous deficient of both the atmosphere and the hydrologic resources. It is formally activated in year $t$ if:

\textbf{Atmospheric Deficit:} Crop water deficit is greater than $0.4$ which is more than 40\% of the crop water requirement:

\[
crop\_water\_deficit > 0.4
\]

\textbf{Hydrological Deficit:} Recharge efficiency is less than $0.30$ (less than 30\% recharge efficiency of the annual rainfall):

\[
recharge\_efficiency < 0.30
\]

Most importantly, a failed year of dual-deficit isn't an irreversible situation. Agricultural systems are resilient to short-term shocks, for example, an isolated El Niño drought event. A CVLE must have the dual-deficit condition in the target year $t$ and in the previous year $t-1$ occur in succession, not randomly:

\[
consec\_dual =
(dual\_deficit[t] == 1)
\land
(dual\_deficit[t-1] == 1)
\]

This constraint of consecutive years provides an effective filtering mechanism that means a CVLE is only registered if environmental safety net has been systematically breached.

\subsubsection{2) The phenological and agro-climatic thresholds}

In addition to the resource deficits mentioned above, the crop has to be in severe physiological stress during year $t$ within the target year. The CVLE framework simulates this by computing three phenological thresholds for specific crops:

\textbf{Sowing Window Miss ($t_1$):} Time lag between sowing date and optimal sowing date of the crop. A delay of the monsoon of more than $0.6$ months (about 18 days) shifts the crop's growing cycle. This delay causes the reproductive stages of the crop to occur in less favourable temperature conditions later in the season and results in a significant reduction in production:

\[
sowing\_window\_miss > 0.6
\]

\textbf{Annual Precipitation ($t_2$):} $< 40\%$ of the crop's specific water requirements are provided by annual precipitation:

\[
crop\_water\_deficit > 0.4
\]

\textbf{Thermal Insufficiency ($t_3$):} Assesses if the amount of thermal energy accumulated during the growing season (April to September) is adequate. If the sum of all thermal units is below the minimum amount needed for completion of the crop maturation (gdd\_min), the crop will not reach reproductive stage:

Once this occurs, gdd\_adequate is set to 0 (or

\[
gdd\_accumulation < gdd\_min
\]

).

\subsubsection{3) CVLE Integration Rule}

A CVLE is formally declared when the consecutive dual-deficit condition is met, and at least 2 of these 3 phenological stress thresholds are exceeded, in year $t$. This multi-threshold formulation is based on the fact that crops can cope with a single stressor (e.g., late sowing date in the presence of sufficient water) but a combination of multiple stressors in the absence of a buffer in the resources results in total loss of viability. The logic is sequentially applied to each city for the 25 year study period, resulting in a binary target label (cvle\_label) for machine learning classification. The formal logic is given in Algorithm 1.


\begin{algorithm}
\caption{CVLE Label Construction}
\label{alg:cvle}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\FOR{each city}
  \FOR{$t = 1$ \TO $T$}
    \STATE $\textit{consec} \leftarrow \textit{dual\_deficit}[t] = 1 \;\wedge\; \textit{dual\_deficit}[t{-}1] = 1$
    \STATE $s \leftarrow \mathbb{1}[\textit{sowing\_window\_miss}[t] > 0.6] + \mathbb{1}[\textit{crop\_water\_deficit}[t] > 0.4] + \mathbb{1}[\textit{gdd\_adequate}[t] = 0]$
    \STATE $\textit{cvle\_label}[t] \leftarrow 1$ \textbf{if} $\textit{consec} \wedge s \geq 2$ \textbf{else} $0$
  \ENDFOR
\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{4D. Model Architectures}

In this study, we make an extensive comparative analysis of eight machine learning and deep learning models for predicting the Crop Viability Loss Events (CVLEs). The models range widely in algorithmic complexity from linear classifiers to cutting edge temporal attention mechanisms. The architectures are chosen to investigate the advantages of sequential modeling, non-linear feature interactions, and global attention mechanisms for the CVLE classification task.

\subsubsection{1) Traditional Machine Learning Baselines}

To set three classic statistical baselines and tree-based ones:

\begin{itemize}

\item \textbf{Logistic Regression (LR):} The L2 (Ridge) penalty was used as the simplest linear baseline to consider the linear separability of the feature space, with the maximum number of iterations allowed as 2000.

\item \textbf{Random Forest (RF):} An ensemble of 300 decision trees that has a maximum tree depth of 6 and a minimum leaf size of 2. RF is strong in the high dimensional feature space with both static and dynamic features.

\item \textbf{Gradient Boosting Decision Trees (GBDT):} We consider two versions of GBDT: XGBoost (XGB) and LightGBM (LGB). Both are set with 300 estimators, max depth set to 6, learning rate of 0.05 and subsampling and feature fraction of 0.8.

\end{itemize}

LR, RF and LGB are trained using class weights to address the extreme class imbalance in the training data, where positive cases make up only 7.6\% of the total. For the sake of addressing the extreme class imbalance in training data, LR, RF, and LGB are trained using class weights, with

\[
scale\_pos\_weight = 1.0
\]

in LR and RF, and

\[
scale\_pos\_weight =
\frac{neg\_count}{pos\_count}
\]

in LGB.

\subsubsection{2) Sequential Deep Learning Models}

We then apply three deep learning architectures for sequence classification, with a lookback window of the last 5 years of the 17 time-varying features:

\begin{itemize}

\item \textbf{Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM):} Two LSTM layers with 64 hidden units and a dropout rate of 0.2 followed by a dense output layer using sigmoid activation.

\item \textbf{Temporal Convolutional Network (TCN):} Causal 1D conv to replace the recurrence, preventing the leakage of future information. The TCN consists of 4 layers, 3 kernel size, 64 hidden channel size, residual connections, dropout rate of 0.2 and exponential dilation factor of 1, 2, 4, and 8.

\item \textbf{Vanilla Transformer:} A sequence classifier composed of sinusoidal positional encodings, two layers of an encoder (model dimension = 64, number of attention heads = 4, feedforward expansion dimension = 256, dropout = 0.1).

\end{itemize}

The parameters of LSTM, TCN, and Transformer are optimized by the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of

\[
1 \times 10^{-3}
\]

and trained for 30 epochs (LSTM) and 20 epochs (TCN and Transformer) using the Binary Cross-Entropy loss function.

\subsubsection{3) Primary Forecasting Model: Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT)}

Lastly, we use the Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT) as our main forecasting model. TFT is cutting-edge design, state-of-the-art attention-based architecture for multi-horizon time-series forecasting. The TFT is different from the sequential classification models, which predict one single binary classification on a 5-year event horizon, while the TFT covers a 3-year horizon in the future, based on a 5-year lookback window.

The TFT is organized to use a hidden size of 64, and four attention heads. An important benefit of the TFT is its use of Gated Residual Networks (GRNs) and Variable Selection Networks (VSNs) which allow the model to focus on and prioritize each of the static categorical variables city and crop, and static real variables FAO GAEZ crop suitability and EECOCROP biological thresholds separately from the 17 time varying variables.

The continuous variables are normalized by using a GroupNormalizer grouped by city with a softplus transformation. In order to measure the confidence of the prediction, the TFT is trained using multi-output quantile regression (QuantileLoss) for seven target quantiles:

\[
0.1,\;0.25,\;0.5,\;0.75,\;0.9
\]

and two quantiles used for calibration.

It has about 115,000 parameters, regularizes with a dropout rate of 0.1, is optimized via Adam (learning rate

\[
1 \times 10^{-3}
\]

), uses a batch size of 16 and early stops after 5 epochs.

\subsection{4E. Train / Validation / Test Split}

To tackle two main problems of climate and agricultural modeling - temporal dependency and class imbalance, the configuration of train, validation and test datasets is designed in this study. The master data set contains the data for 10 major cities in India for 25 years (2000 to 2024). The high temporal autocorrelation of climate time series makes the common random splitting and random k-fold cross-validation techniques very unsuitable. Random partitioning of the data would result in significant temporal leakage and lookahead bias in which a model learned from data from years $t-1$ and $t+1$ might have been able to predict the state of year $t$ without having to learn the causal dynamics. To test the models for their capacity to predict future climates that are not part of their training, we use a very strict chronological temporal split, without any shuffling of data.

This temporal partition is then mapped to ``sliding sequence windows'' for sequential deep learning architectures. The length of the lookback sequence 5 years means the first predictions must start in the 6th year of the temporal split of the training period (2005) which helps avoid sequence lookbacks including information over temporal partition boundaries or future validation \& test data.

\subsubsection{1) Dataset Partitioning}

Partitions are chronological:

\begin{itemize}

\item \textbf{The year range for the training Set is 2000-2018,} with 16 positive CVLE cases. This split is applied for the baseline model, training the deep models and fine tuning model parameters.

\item \textbf{Validation Set:} includes the years 2019-2021 (30 samples with 3 samples containing positive cases). With hyperparameter optimization and early stopping, a validation split is used, yielding a total of 220 samples (19 positives).

\item \textbf{Test Set:} Contains 30 samples test cases covering the year 2022 to 2024 with exactly 1 positive CVLE case. This final split is not accessed during any of the training or hyperparameter tuning steps, and is only used in an unbiased model evaluation step.

\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{2) Class Imbalance and Evaluation Metrics}

One of the main challenges of this dataset is that it is highly imbalanced. The number of city-year combinations with positive CVLE is 19 over a total of 250 city-years studied (a prevalence of 7.6\%) across the entire 25-year study. The unbalance is even harsher for the test set at a ratio of 1:29. The test split is the true chronological sequence of climate events, but this is a known limitation as only a single positive event occurs between 2022 and 2024, making AUC and precision metrics very sensitive to small changes in predictions. If the model would predict that there is no viability loss event, then it would be accurate on the test set with 96.67\%, but would not be able to detect any of the actual viability loss events. To overcome the class imbalance and avoid such models becoming overfitted to the majority class, we take three key actions:

\begin{itemize}

\item \textbf{Class Weighting During training:} Yes. We use the balanced class weight configuration for Logistic Regression, Random Forest and LightGBM, which will automatically apply class weights inversely to the class frequencies. In the case of XGBoost, we use the ratio of negative to positive training examples for the parameter

\[
scale\_pos\_weight
\]

that determines positive scaling.

\item \textbf{Decision threshold optimization:} We do the decision threshold optimization. We opt for a grid search over the validation set instead of the usual threshold of 0.5, to get the best decision boundary in terms of the maximum macro-averaged F1 score. This helps to ensure that the positive class is evaluated equally.

\item \textbf{Distribution-Insensitive Metrics:} We emphasize metrics which are not strongly affected by class distribution. We focus on Area under the Receiver Operating Characteristic (AUC-ROC), macro-averaged F1-scores and Brier scores. The Brier score is one indicator of model calibration that is the mean squared difference between the predicted probabilities and actual probabilities.

\end{itemize}

\subsection{4F. Threshold Optimisation and Evaluation Metrics}

The models' predictive ability in this study needs to be assessed in a multi-faceted way, as a measure of just classification accuracy is insufficient. The data set, Crop Viability Loss Event (CVLE), is extremely imbalanced, with positive samples accounting for just 7.6\% of the master data set, and only one positive sample in the test set, making the standard accuracy a very deceptive measure. A naive classifier that classifies each and every test sample as ``no event'' would get an erroneously high accuracy rate of 96.67\% and at the same time be unable to detect the agricultural crisis. We use four important evaluation metrics: Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic curve (AUC-ROC), macro-averaged F1-score, Brier score and overall classification accuracy to provide a rigorous and balanced evaluation.

\subsubsection{1) Relationship between Classification and Discrimination Metrics}

\textbf{Macro-Averaged F1-Score (Macro-F1):} Our main optimization score. While micro-averaged F1 is the average of the F1 scores of the individual classes, it is weighted by the number of classes, macro-average F1 is computed separately for each class and then averaged together. This is the case for the positive class (event) and the negative class (non-event). This will make it a strong indicator of performance under severe class imbalance since it will heavily punish the model if it doesn't predict the rare positive class.

\textbf{Area Under the ROC Curve (AUC-ROC):} Measures how well the model performs on all possible decision boundaries. It is based on the probability that a randomly selected positive class sample will have a higher risk score than a randomly selected negative class sample and thus provides an independent assessment of the quality of the classifiers, without relying on a threshold.

\subsubsection{2) CVA Score and Signatures of Skill}

AUC-ROC is a measure of discrimination, whereas climate adaptation decisions need well-calibrated probabilities of risk. For example, if a model projects a 70\% chance of the loss of crop viability, then in the historical scenarios, the loss of crop viability should occur around 70\% of the time. Brier score is used to assess the calibration of probability. The Brier score is given by the mean squared error of the predicted probabilities, which can be expressed mathematically as:

\[
\text{Brier}
=
\frac{1}{N}
\sum_{i=1}^{N}
(p_i - y_i)^2
\]

where $N$ is the number of all samples, $p_i$ is the predicted probability of a CVLE and $y_i$ is the ground truth binary label. Brier scores are between 0 and 1, with 0 being the best prediction and 1 being the worst prediction. This calibration check is particularly important to ensure that model predictions are reliable estimates of the actual physical probability of a policy's outcome and are applicable to policy planning.

\subsubsection{3) Decision Threshold Grid Search}

We optimize the decision threshold to map the continuous probability values to the binary classification labels, in order to fill the gap. The default decision threshold for a machine learning classifiers is 0.5. In a very imbalanced classification, however, this threshold is very sub-optimal and often causes deep neural networks and tree ensembles to predict all cases as the majority class, with a positive recall of 0.

In order to avoid this collapse, we make an evaluation over the validation set for each of the 8 models. The thresholds used are the ones between 0.01 and 0.99 with a step size of 0.01. Macro Average F1 on validation set for each step. This grid search will ensure a balance between false positives and negatives that will reflect the high cost of missing a CVLE (false negative) and the cost of agricultural intervention being made unnecessarily (false positive).

The value of the threshold which maximizes the validation macro-F1 is chosen as the optimal threshold of the model. The optimum threshold is then customized and used on the independent test set (2022--2024) to determine the final classification metrics that are reported. We optimize thresholds only using the validation split and test using the isolated test split to avoid data leakage and to get a fair and generalized view of the performance of the model.

\subsection{Uncertainty Quantification}
An uncertainty quantification framework designed for both ensemble tree-based models and deep learning models is used to evaluate the accuracy of model predictions. 
For deep temporal models (LSTM, TCN, and Vanilla Transformer), Monte Carlo (MC) dropout is utilized. The models are maintained in training mode during test-time inference, enabling the dropout layers to continue functioning. For every input sequence, 50 stochastic forward passes are used to produce a set of predicted probabilities $\{p^{(m)}_t\}_{m=1}^M$. The empirical mean is used to estimate the final prediction probability:
\begin{equation}
\mu_t = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{m=1}^M p^{(m)}_t
\end{equation}
and the standard deviation measures the predictive uncertainty of the model:
\begin{equation}
\sigma_t = \sqrt{\frac{1}{M} \sum_{m=1}^M \left(p^{(m)}_t - \mu_t\right)^2}
\end{equation}

Tree-level variance is employed for the Random Forest model, which contains 200 separate decision tree estimators. The standard deviation of the class probabilities predicted across the individual trees is used to calculate the predictive uncertainty:
\begin{equation}
\sigma_t^{\text{RF}} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=1}^K \left(p^{(k)}_t - \mu_t^{\text{RF}}\right)^2}
\end{equation}
where $p^{(k)}_t$ is the probability predicted by the $k$-th tree.

Using the estimated standard deviation, the $90\%$ predictive confidence interval width for each sample is derived under a normal approximation:
\begin{equation}
\text{Width}_t = 2 \times 1.645 \times \sigma_t
\end{equation}

To evaluate model calibration (how closely the predicted probabilities correspond to empirical frequencies), the Expected Calibration Error (ECE) is computed using $B = 10$ equal-width bins spanning $[0,1]$:
\begin{equation}
\text{ECE} = \sum_{b=1}^B \frac{|B_b|}{N} \left| \text{acc}(B_b) - \text{conf}(B_b) \right|
\end{equation}
where $N$ is the total number of test samples, $B_b$ is the set of samples in the $b$-th bin, $\text{acc}(B_b)$ is the true positive rate in $B_b$, and $\text{conf}(B_b)$ is the average predicted confidence in $B_b$. ECE is evaluated on the test set alongside the Brier score.

\subsection{Feature Group Ablation}
A feature group ablation study is conducted to examine the models' sensitivity to various data modalities and determine which drivers predominate in crop viability loss predictions. The 17 time-varying and 5 static features are divided into five different, physically motivated subsets:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Climate}: Basic atmospheric conditions that indicate warmth and moisture, such as mean annual temperature ($T_{\text{mean}}$), mean maximum temperature ($T_{\text{max}}$), annual precipitation ($P_{\text{annual}}$), wind speed ($W_{\text{speed}}$), humidity ($RH$), and the number of dry months ($N_{\text{dry\_months}}$).
    \item \textbf{Phenology}: Measures of crop-specific timing and thermal satisfaction, such as growing degree days adequacy ($GDD_{\text{adequate}}$), growing degree days accumulation ($GDD_{\text{acc}}$), sowing window miss ($S_{\text{window\_miss}}$), and monsoon onset day of year ($DOY_{\text{onset}}$).
    \item \textbf{Hydrology}: Measures of the availability and demand of subsurface water, such as groundwater depth before ($D_{\text{pre}}$) and after the monsoon ($D_{\text{post}}$), depletion rate ($D_{\text{rate}}$), recharge efficiency ($R_{\text{eff}}$), crop water deficit ($W_{\text{deficit}}$), and the dual-deficit indicator ($D_{\text{deficit}}$).
    \item \textbf{Static Context}: Baseline crop suitability class ($S_{\text{GAEZ}}$) and geographic baselines that reflect the city's general climate classification ($K_{\text{zone}}$).
    \item \textbf{All Features}: The unified feature set combining all 17 features.
\end{enumerate}

Three representative architectures---Random Forest (ensemble machine learning), XGBoost (gradient boosting), and LSTM (recurrent neural network)---are evaluated by training them from scratch on each feature group independently. The performance is assessed using the test set AUC-ROC.

\subsection{Pairwise Statistical Significance}
A pairwise Wilcoxon signed-rank test is conducted on the per-sample Brier losses across the test set (30 samples) to ascertain whether the variations in predictive performance among the models are statistically significant. Across the eight models, the 28 pairwise comparisons (8 choose 2) are assessed at a significance level of 0.05. 

Several important conclusions are drawn from the statistical tests:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Logistic Regression is statistically superior to the LSTM ($p = 3.79 \times 10^{-6}$) and Vanilla Transformer ($p = 9.52 \times 10^{-4}$), indicating a high degree of linear separability in the engineered feature space. On this test set, however, neither the Random Forest model nor the TCN exhibit any statistically significant differences ($p = 0.2534$ and $p = 0.0919$, respectively).
    \item The Random Forest baseline outperforms XGBoost ($p = 0.0099$) and LightGBM ($p = 0.0093$) statistically, suggesting that under extreme class imbalance, its class-weight balancing and bagging mechanism produce more stable probability predictions than gradient boosting.
    \item The LSTM ($p = 2.76 \times 10^{-6}$) and XGBoost ($p = 0.0449$) are statistically inferior to the TCN among the deep learning models. However, the TCN does not show a statistically significant difference compared to the Random Forest baseline ($p = 0.3930$).
    \item The TFT model, when evaluated on its optimized predictions, shows statistically significant differences compared to LightGBM ($p = 0.0016$) and LSTM ($p = 2.76 \times 10^{-6}$), but is statistically indistinguishable from Logistic Regression ($p = 0.4161$) and Random Forest.
\end{enumerate}
Overall, the Wilcoxon signed-rank tests confirm that simpler models and causal convolutional models are statistically superior to standard recurrent models and boosting algorithms for Crop Viability Loss Event detection on this dataset.

The zero false detection rate in control cities validates the dual-deficit formulation: the dual atmospheric+hydrological threshold requirement effectively suppresses false positives in cities where groundwater recharge remains above the 0.30 efficiency threshold even during below-average monsoon years.

\subsection{Why Simple Models Outperform Deep Models}

The unified performance results (Table~\ref{tab:unified_performance} and Fig.~\ref{fig:model_comparison}) reveal a consistent and somewhat counter-intuitive finding: model complexity is inversely related to test-set AUC-ROC on this dataset. Logistic Regression achieves a perfect AUC-ROC = 1.0000, and Random Forest achieves AUC-ROC = 0.9655, both substantially outperforming the most architecturally complex model---the Temporal Fusion Transformer---which collapses to AUC-ROC = 0.0690.

Two complementary mechanisms explain this outcome. First, the feature ablation study demonstrates that the CVLE classification problem is largely linearly separable in the static context subspace: the two features \texttt{gaez\_baseline\_class} and \texttt{koppen\_zone\_enc} alone achieve AUC = 0.9828 for RF and XGBoost. This high-dimensional near-linear structure is exploited immediately by Logistic Regression, which is mathematically equivalent to fitting a linear decision boundary in the 22-dimensional feature space. Because the static baseline suitability score already encodes the long-run agroclimatic constraints of each city-crop pair, the positive class (CVLE events) forms a near-separable cluster in this static feature subspace, and no temporal modeling is needed to locate it.

Second, the dataset size ($n = 250$) is fundamentally inadequate for deep temporal architectures to generalise. The LSTM, TCN, and Transformer contain between 8,000 and 30,000 trainable parameters respectively, and the TFT contains approximately 115,000. With only 190 training samples (16 positives), these models are operating far below the sample-to-parameter ratio required for reliable convergence on rare event classification. The result is that the models either overfit the training majority class or learn spurious short-term temporal correlations that do not transfer to the 2022--2024 test window. This is consistent with the Rolnick et al. \cite{rolnick2022} observation that data scarcity and class imbalance are the primary barriers to deep temporal models in agricultural applications.

\subsection{Why the Temporal Fusion Transformer Fails}

The TFT's near-zero test AUC-ROC (0.069) warrants specific analysis because TFT is state-of-the-art on large-scale time-series forecasting benchmarks. Three structural incompatibilities between the TFT design and the CVLE task are identified.

\textit{First}, the TFT is optimised using multi-output QuantileLoss across seven target quantiles ($q \in \{0.1, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 0.9\}$ plus two calibration quantiles). This loss function is designed for continuous-valued multi-horizon regression---electricity demand, retail sales, traffic flow---where the training signal is dense and the target distribution is smooth. Binary event classification provides a degenerate training signal for quantile regression: the target is either 0 or 1, collapsing the full 7-quantile distribution into a degenerate two-point mass. The resulting gradient landscape does not produce meaningful quantile separation for rare binary events.

\textit{Second}, the TFT's approximately 115,000 parameters represent a severe over-parameterisation relative to the 190-row training set. The model's Gated Residual Networks (GRNs) and Variable Selection Networks (VSNs) are designed to learn which variables are informative for which time steps---a meaningful capability on datasets with hundreds of thousands of samples. On 190 samples, these mechanisms cannot converge to stable variable selection weights, causing the model to ignore predictive features entirely.

\textit{Third}, the TFT was implemented using the pytorch-forecasting library's \texttt{TimeSeriesDataSet} infrastructure, which treats the task as multi-horizon regression with a 3-year prediction window. The implicit assumption of temporal smoothness and continuous output breaks down for a binary event indicator with a 7.6\% prevalence rate, where the positive class is both rare and non-smooth in time.

These findings serve as a practical warning for practitioners: TFT should not be applied to small longitudinal datasets ($n < 500$) with binary targets without architectural modifications, such as replacing QuantileLoss with Binary Cross-Entropy and pre-training on related datasets via transfer learning.

\subsection{Role of Static versus Dynamic Features}

The feature ablation study (Table~\ref{tab:ablation_results}) and SHAP analysis (Fig.~\ref{fig:shap_importance}) together provide a consistent and physically interpretable account of the information structure underlying CVLE prediction.

The static context group---comprising only the baseline FAO GAEZ crop suitability score (\texttt{gaez\_baseline\_class}) and the current-year Köppen-Geiger zone encoding (\texttt{koppen\_zone\_enc})---achieves AUC-ROC $\geq$ 0.9828 for both RF and XGBoost, and 0.9655 for LSTM. This near-complete predictive power from just two features reflects a fundamental agroclimatic insight: the FAO GAEZ rainfed suitability score (calibrated on the 1981--2010 historical baseline) already encodes the long-run compatibility between a city's climatological regime and the assigned crop's physiological requirements. When a city's current Köppen zone departs significantly from its 1981--2010 baseline zone, the combination of a low suitability score and a ``wrong'' current zone provides an almost unambiguous CVLE signal.

Time-varying features contribute marginal incremental AUC for tree-based models when added to the static context: RF AUC increases from 0.9828 (static only) to 0.9655 (all features combined), which is actually a slight decrease, indicating that the dynamic features introduce minor noise on this small test set. However, dynamic features are not irrelevant---they are the primary signal for recurrent architectures operating on the hydrology feature group alone (LSTM AUC = 0.8621 with hydrology only, equal to its all-features performance). This split reveals that the two model families operate on fundamentally different information channels: tree-based models exploit the static suitability regime, while recurrent models exploit the temporal trend in groundwater depletion.

\subsection{Groundwater as the Differentiating Signal for Recurrent Models}

The most instructive finding of the ablation study is that the hydrology feature group---comprising pre-monsoon groundwater depth, annual depletion rate, monsoon recharge efficiency, crop water deficit, and the dual-deficit indicator---achieves an LSTM AUC-ROC of 0.8621, the highest AUC of any single feature group for sequential architectures. This result matches the LSTM's performance when trained on all 17 time-varying features (AUC = 0.8621), indicating that the hydrology group alone carries essentially all of the temporal information exploitable by the LSTM for CVLE detection.

This finding has a clear physical interpretation. Groundwater depletion is an integrating process: the pre-monsoon depth in year $t$ reflects the cumulative imbalance between extraction and recharge over the preceding years, and the annual depletion rate $\Delta Jan\_depth$ captures the year-over-year trend in aquifer health. Unlike atmospheric variables that are highly variable year to year (responding to ENSO, monsoon variability), groundwater depletion trends are persistent and carry a lagged, cumulative signal that recurrent networks with a 5-year lookback window can exploit. The LSTM's ability to maintain cell state across multiple timesteps is directly suited to learning this multi-year depletion trajectory.

In contrast, atmospheric climate features (annual temperature, rainfall, wind, humidity) achieve LSTM AUC = 0.2069---barely above chance---and phenological features achieve only 0.2414. This collapse of recurrent models on atmospheric-only inputs confirms that short-term atmospheric variability, absent the multi-year groundwater context, is insufficient for CVLE event detection. The implication for deployment is that continuity of CGWB groundwater monitoring is operationally critical: any interruption to the observation well network will degrade the recurrent model's predictive performance while leaving tree-based models unaffected.

\subsection{Limitations and Validity Threats}

Several important limitations constrain the generalisability of the VegShift results and should be disclosed for replication and deployment:

\textit{Small test set with extreme imbalance}: The test set contains only 30 samples (10 cities, 3 years) with a single positive case (Lucknow sugarcane, 2023). This makes AUC-ROC and precision estimates statistically unstable: a single misclassified sample changes the AUC by more than 0.03 units. The perfect AUC = 1.0000 for Logistic Regression should be interpreted cautiously, as it reflects performance on a single positive event. A larger evaluation set spanning more future years would provide more robust model comparison.

\textit{CGWB data termination}: The CGWB observation network provides data through 2022 only. Groundwater values for 2023--2024 (the test period) were imputed from the city-group mean depletion rate, introducing a known measurement gap that affects the hydrological features used by recurrent models. This limitation will be resolved as the CGWB dataset is extended.

\textit{Jaipur groundwater proxy}: Jaipur has no CGWB observation wells within the 50 km aggregation radius. Its groundwater features were derived from the five nearest available wells regardless of distance, flagged as \texttt{gw\_imputed=2}. The spatial representativeness of these proxy wells for the Jaipur basin is uncertain, and the city's groundwater estimates carry higher uncertainty than all other cities.

\textit{Humidity estimation}: Relative humidity was not measured directly in the Open-Meteo dataset. It was derived via back-calculation from Steadman's apparent temperature inversion, which is an indirect approximation. While climatological validation against known regional means (Mumbai $\sim$90\%, Chennai $\sim$88\%, Delhi $\sim$74\%, Jaipur $\sim$65\%) confirms plausibility, the estimated values introduce systematic uncertainty for cities with complex urban heat island effects.

\textit{Threshold subjectivity}: The three CVLE thresholds---crop water deficit $> 0.4$, recharge efficiency $< 0.30$, sowing window miss $> 0.6$---are domain-informed from agrophysiology literature but are not empirically derived from observed crop yield loss records. Alternative threshold choices would change the 19 positive labels and potentially alter the model ranking. Future work should calibrate thresholds against district-level agricultural census yield data.

\textit{Static GAEZ baseline}: The FAO GAEZ v4 suitability scores are calibrated on the 1981--2010 historical climate baseline and do not capture post-2010 suitability shifts driven by continued warming. As the GAEZ classification falls increasingly out of date, the dominant static features will lose predictive power in future deployment windows, increasing the relative importance of dynamic features.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

This paper introduced VegShift, the first formal framework for timestamping Crop Viability Loss Events (CVLEs) tied to Köppen-Geiger climate zone transitions, validated across 10 major Indian cities over a 25-year study period (2000--2024) using three heterogeneous data sources: daily atmospheric climate data from Open-Meteo, seasonal groundwater observations from 2,759 CGWB wells, and FAO GAEZ v4 rainfed crop suitability rasters.

The CVLE definition, based on dual-deficit consecutive years---simultaneous atmospheric rainfall failure and groundwater recharge collapse---combined with at least two of three crop-specific phenological stress thresholds, provides a physically grounded and formally auditable event timestamping mechanism. The framework detected 19 CVLE events distributed across 7 at-risk cities, with zero false detections in 3 climatically stable control cities (Pune, Kolkata, Mumbai), validating the dual-threshold formulation as a robust discriminator between temporary drought years and permanent viability loss transitions.

A systematic comparison of eight machine learning and deep learning models demonstrates that simpler models substantially outperform complex architectures on this 250-sample dataset. \textit{Random Forest is the recommended model for operational deployment}: it achieves AUC-ROC = 0.9655, Macro-F1 = 0.8246, and the best-calibrated uncertainty estimates (ECE = 0.045, tree variance), while remaining interpretable and computationally efficient for deployment in agricultural early-warning systems. Logistic Regression achieves a perfect AUC-ROC = 1.0000, confirming that the CVLE classification problem is nearly linearly separable in the static context subspace defined by the FAO GAEZ suitability score and current Köppen zone encoding.

The Temporal Fusion Transformer (AUC-ROC = 0.069) fails on this dataset due to the incompatibility between QuantileLoss multi-horizon regression and binary event classification at $n = 250$ samples---a cautionary finding for practitioners considering TFT for small geospatial datasets.

Future research directions include: (1) extending the geographic scope to all 36 Indian states to increase sample size and improve deep model generalisability; (2) calibrating CVLE thresholds against district-level crop yield census records to move from domain-informed to empirically-derived event boundaries; (3) incorporating post-2022 CGWB groundwater data to close the measurement gap in the test period; and (4) investigating transfer learning from larger agroclimatic datasets to initialise the TFT and LSTM weights before fine-tuning on the CVLE corpus.
