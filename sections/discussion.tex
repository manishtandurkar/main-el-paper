\section{Discussion}

The experimental results demonstrate that ADRDS effectively integrates behavioral modeling, hybrid machine learning, and interactive visualization to provide early and interpretable ransomware detection. This section discusses the implications of these results, key design trade-offs, and the limitations of the current system.

\subsection{Effectiveness of Behavioral Modeling}

The results highlight the importance of behavior-based analysis for ransomware detection. By structuring detection around a stage-aware behavioral taxonomy, ADRDS is able to identify malicious activity during early phases such as file enumeration and pre-encryption preparation. This capability is particularly valuable, as it enables detection before irreversible damage occurs. The findings reinforce prior observations that behavioral indicators such as burst file access patterns and entropy variation are more reliable than static signatures in modern ransomware scenarios.

\subsection{Benefits of Hybrid Detection}

The evaluation confirms that combining supervised classification with unsupervised anomaly detection yields more robust performance than either approach alone. Supervised models provide strong accuracy for known ransomware behaviors, while the anomaly detection component improves resilience against novel or evolving attack patterns. The hybrid risk fusion mechanism reduces false positives and enables earlier detection, demonstrating the complementary strengths of the two learning paradigms.

\subsection{Interpretability and User Interaction}

A key contribution of ADRDS lies in its emphasis on transparency and interactivity. By mapping detection outcomes to behavioral stages and visualizing risk progression in real time, the system allows users to understand not only \emph{that} an alert was generated, but also \emph{why} it was triggered. This interpretability is critical for educational settings and supports analyst trust in automated detection systems. The interactive feedback loop further enables exploration of detection thresholds and attacker behavior variations, providing practical insight into detection trade-offs.

\subsection{Performance and Practicality}

The observed sub-second end-to-end latency demonstrates that ADRDS is suitable for real-time monitoring and interactive demonstrations. The lightweight feature extraction pipeline and modular architecture contribute to this responsiveness, making the system practical for exploratory analysis and classroom use. While ADRDS is not intended as a production-grade endpoint protection solution, its performance characteristics indicate potential for adaptation to real-world defensive research environments.

\subsection{Limitations}

Despite its strengths, ADRDS has several limitations. First, the evaluation relies primarily on simulated ransomware behavior, which may not capture all nuances of real-world malware execution. Second, the system assumes visibility into host-level telemetry, which may not always be available in constrained environments. Finally, while the hybrid detection framework improves robustness, threshold selection remains a non-trivial challenge and may require tuning for different deployment contexts.

\subsection{Implications for Future Research}

The discussion suggests several directions for future work. Incorporating additional telemetry sources such as system call traces and network traffic could enhance detection fidelity. Exploring more advanced deep learning architectures, including transformer-based or graph-based models, may further improve detection of complex behavioral relationships. Additionally, integrating explainable AI techniques could provide deeper insight into model decisions and strengthen user trust in automated ransomware defense systems.
